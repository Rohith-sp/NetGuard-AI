NetGuard AI: A Real-Time Explainable IoT Intrusion Detection SystemRohith S PanchamukhiDept. of CSERV College of Engineering Bengaluru, India rohithsp.cs24@rvce.edu.inRohith Kumar LDept. of CSERV College of Engineering Bengaluru, India rohithkumarl.cs24@rvce.edu.inRahul A SDept. of CSERV College of Engineering Bengaluru, India rahulas.cs24@rvce.edu.inAbstract—As the number of connected devices on the Internet rapidly increases, our networks are increasingly exposed to threats. To ensure security, our network approach must be significantly improved in the future. In this study, we developed NetGuard AI, a complete Intrusion Detection System (IDS) for IoT running on physical microcontrollers. We conducted live monitoring and set up a network with three ESP32 nodes. Through conducting this experiment, I discovered that most of the traditional ML models trained on the flows have severe lim-itations in terms of detection abilities for zero-flow and payload attacks, such as the inability to detect the slow-rate attack with zero-flow data and the inability to detect data poisoning with the normal packet timing. Thus, in response to this issue, we proposed the Two-Stage Hybrid Detection Pipeline, which utilizes statistical profiling with Exponential Moving Average (EMA) Z-scores and long-time estimation of packet inter-arrival time (IAT) in Stage 1 and a Random Forest classifier for volumetric, replay, topic-bombing, and evasive attacks in Stage 2. As we obtained a 99.20% accuracy on seven different behavioral states, and since machine learning is somewhat of a ”black-box” we wanted to be able to interpret why these decisions were made. Therefore, we designed three levels of Explainable AI (XAI) stack, where the local decision explanation is performed by SHapley Additive exPlanations(SHAP), and these SHAP values are consumed by a Large Language Model (LLM)-powered Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline that generates human-readable incident reports through a secure API cascade. These findings, along with live data, explanations, and physical node states, are presented on our custom-built web-based SOC.Index Terms—Intrusion Detection, Explainable AI, Internet of Things, Machine Learning, Security Operations Center, Random Forest, RAG, Z-Score, Hybrid PipelineIntroductionToday’s IoT connects the physical and digital world for intelligent automation and monitoring [1]. The catch is, most edge devices are small and don’t have lots of resources. They are very limited in terms of memory, and they don’t have that much processing power even for simple things. So we can’t easily attach lots of encryption or use standard security software on them [2]. For this reason, IoT environments are the next biggest target, with everything ranging from enormous denial of service floods and unobtrusive slow-rate attacks to attackers altering sensor payloads. [3].In a typical IDS, one solution to this problem would be looking for a specific attack signature. Of course this requires that you’ve seen this attack before; otherwise it can be easilycircumvented [4]. ML can find the odd network behaviors far better as it examines the intricate patterns [5].However, ML models are essentially black boxes. ML models may tell you there is an attack taking place but will not provide the reasons for it to do so. We interviewed individuals within a SOC, and it became clear that the output of a ”yes/no” alert is not sufficient for individuals working within a SOC. They require an immediate explanation to the occurring issues in order to mitigate the incident effectively [6].We also observed that traditional supervised ML models-which generally examine traffic within 10 second intervals-possess significant limitations. A Slow Rate attack could simply bypass such a model by only submitting a malicious packet every 15-30 seconds; it would be completely missed since the 10-second windows in between packets would appear empty and otherwisenormal. This also occurs in the case of Data Poisoning attacks, where the attacker submits false sensor readings but maintainseactly the same packet timing as the device it aims to mimic; thus, the flow features are identical. We developed NetGuard AI to address some of these shortcomings. It’s a combined hardware and software system which we created to act as a real-time IDS for the IoT. Unlike many papers which test only offline against historical datasets [7], we set up a physical network of ESP32 microcontrollers sending MQTT messages through a cloud broker in order toprocess live traffic.The primary contribution that we make is the Two-Stage Hybrid Detection Pipeline. For Stage 1 we have a completely unsupervised Statistical Profiler that focuses on watching packet gaps in the short to medium term so as to not let those Slow Rate attacks through, and we include an EMA Z-score check on the actual sensor readings to mitigate Data Poisoning. A packet that survives this check without triggering it will go into Stage 2: a Random Forest Classifier that operates on 10 features we designed for the flow; I have tested this algorithm and find it to be very accurate with a 99.20% accuracy across 7 different states.One of the key features that we implemented was a three-tiered Explainable AI (XAI) stack. We take SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) locally and feed them into a RAG system, using a LLM [8]. Essentially, the system takes the complex math from the model and writes it out as an easilyreadable summary incident report. We stream all of this directly to a web dashboard so you are able to monitor the network live.Related WorkThere’s been significant research into IoT security, Machine learning, and Explainable AI recently. In our project, we aim to build upon, and possibly improve, four key aspects from the literature.IoT Security and Intrusion DetectionWe all are aware that some protocols have very peculiar weaknesses such as MQTT and CoAP. A recent security analysis on vulnerabilities [3] has revealed that MQTT brokers are susceptible to both packet flooding and payload injection attacks. Early IoT IDS systems were primarily designed as simple rule based systems [13]. Such systems are poor in detecting zero-day and slow rate attacks [15] and possess poor false negative rates. To overcome such deficiencies, in our proposed NetGuard AI system, we integrated behavioral flow features with statistical check on payloads. We consider that such approaches help us to protect from both network level and application level threats.Machine Learning in Network SecurityEnsemble learning for network security is extremely effec-tive. If you look at the papers that evaluated performance on benchmark data, decision tree approaches like Random Forests and Gradient Boosting are consistently suggested as the best option when it comes to dealing with highly noisy, high dimensional data obtained from network traffic [4]. And hybrid/ensemble models have already been demonstrated to perform better than individual models when trying to detect ”abnormal” IoT anomalies [11]. What bothered us in existing literature was that all of these methods were evaluated against static, offline data only. We decided to see how well these methods performed in the real world, where we deployed and tested against real, live traffic obtained directly from hardware and covered 7 states.Explainable AI (XAI) in CybersecurityIn the context of security, nobody would ever rely on a black-box ML model in an important security-related setting. Things turned out to be different, since SHAP feature attri-bution [5] became publicly available, it gave a mathematical guarantee on the detection of the adversarial attacks. Recently, people began to apply SHAP to the domain of IDS like: measuring a multi-class drone intrusion [14] or performing federated learning in a heterogeneous IoT setting [6]. Never-theless, the major problem relies in the computation time to compute such explanations at runtime (which would lead to the blocking of the system). To overcome this problem, we executed theTreeExplainerin an asynchronous thread-pool and computed the local explanation as quickly as possible without having the FastAPI server blocking.LLMs and RAG in Security OperationsThis approach of integrating LLMs into SOC operations is still relatively novel. Previous automated reporting tools tend to be just template fillers and do not provide the necessary context. In recent work, several researchers have demonstrated that LLMs can actually assist in the analysis of threat intelli-gence and reading logs [8]–[10]. We simply can’t provide raw network data as input to an LLM, because the result will be entirely hallucinated. We combat this issue by building on top of a RAG architecture [7]. We pin down the prompt given to the LLM using real mathematics from the SHAP values, the current feature vectors, and a local knowledge base. It acts like an automated Tier-1 analyst but stays factual.Design and ImplementationWe divided the NetGuard AI system into three major parts: physical hardware edge, Python FastAPI backend, and Next.js front-end. To ensure speed of data transit, simple MQTT pub/sub mechanism was employed. As you can see in Fig how it is all wired up together: 1.Hardware Edge LayerFor the physical edge, I set up three different ESP32 microcontrollers (Tensilica Xtensa LX6) talking over Wi-Fi:ESP321 (Environmental Node): This one connects to a DHT22 sensor and sends out temperature and humidity data. We programmed it to publish every 2 to 5 seconds. I also added some random jitter to the timing because real IoT devices almost never sync up perfectly.ESP322 (Ambient Node): I wired up a Light Dependent Resistor (LDR) in a simple voltage divider here to track ambient light. It has a 16×2 I2C LCD and a physical push-button hooked up to a debounced interrupt on GPIO 14. When you press the button, it cycles through seven differentattack modes, like DoS Floods, Replay attacks, and Data Poisoning. It also listens to the netguard/alerts MQTT topic. If it hears that an attack was detected, a hardware interrupt fires off, triggering a loud piezo buzzer and flashing a red LED for a few seconds.Backend Processing EngineThe backend was written in python with FastAPI and we have a Paho-MQTT client just lying dormant and listening on the netguard/# wildcard topic over the public HiveMQ broker. All the packets coming in to the queue are just chucked into a 60s memory buffer using a collections.deque.The async loop runs every 5s, taking the last 10s of packets, and feeding that through the feature extraction, and through our Two-Stage Hybrid Detection Pipeline. Plus, it’s completely useless if the traffic is encrypted. That’s why we focused on behavioral flow features instead. We look at a 10-second sliding window of traffic and shift it forward every 5 seconds. For each of these windows, we pull out 10 specific stats:Packet Count (N ): How many packets arrived.PacketRate(Rpkt):Packetspersecond (N/windowsize).Mean Inter-Arrival Time (µiat): The average gap between packets in milliseconds.Standard Deviation of Inter-Arrival Time (σiat): How much those gaps vary.Minimum Inter-Arrival Time (miniat): The shortest gap we saw.Maximum Inter-Arrival Time (maxiat): The longest gap.Duplicate Ratio (Rdup): The fraction of sequence num-bers that were repeats (Ndup/N ).Sequence Increment Mean (µ∆seq): The average jump between sequence numbers (seqi+1 − seqi).Sequence Increment Standard Deviation (σ∆seq): Thevariance of those sequence jumps.Unique Modes (Muniq): The number of different device state strings in the window.Edge Case Handling:This was a little more of a pain to do for the Slow-Rate attack type. The attacks normally send only a single packet ever 15 or 30 seconds. Since the algorithm had a 10sec window that only captures one packet, then trying to take the mean and variance of the numbers would fail. To overcome this I have built the algorithm to detect when there is only a single packet and input the entire window length (10, 000ms) as the inter-arrival time. Distorting the math in this way gives the perfect fingerprint for the model to easily determine stealthy probes. You can clearly see all of our classes plotted with our training data. Local SHAP explanation for a Data Poisoning classification, showcasing the deterministic rule-engine override.Conclusion and Future WorkNetGuard AI demonstrated that ML based IoT IDS can actually be run with close to perfect accuracy (99.20%) on real-world live edge hardware and explain its every decision. Instead of parsing the packet payload for unnecessary infor-mation, focusing on behavior within small windows of time was shown to be much more effective. Our Two-Stage Hybrid Detection Pipeline identified both the ”loud” and ”stealthy” attacks. The statistical stage successfully detected the Slow-Rate gaps and Data Poisoning abnormalities while the Random Forest successfully detected DoS Floods, Replay, Topic Bombsand Evasion. Combined with our XAI stack and having an LLM generate the reports, this significantly offloads the SOC analysts and actually makes raw data useful.Further work would involve having a larger number of physical nodes for the ESP32 attacker to better simulate the conditions of a DDoS attack and investigate specific MQTT exploits such as sending Malformed Topic.
